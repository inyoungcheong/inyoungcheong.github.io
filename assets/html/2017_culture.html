I. Introduction

Freedom of art is widely protected under Article 22 of the Constitution, and there are few regulations governing the actions of cultural and artistic professionals because, compared to other industries, there are fewer issues with unfair trade, consumer protection, and environmental-safety. Laws such as the "Cultural Arts Promotion Act," "Law on Revitalizing Cultural Arts Sponsorship," "Craft Culture Industry Promotion Act," and "Cartoon Promotion Act" are primarily focused on nurturing and supporting their respective fields, including establishing government development plans, conducting surveys, providing financial support, and establishing institutions. Therefore, unlike the typical three-party regulatory structure of 'regulator (government) - regulated party (market actor) - beneficiary of regulation (consumers/other market actors)', this becomes a two-party structure of 'supporter - support recipient'

However, recently two major legislative needs have emerged in the cultural arts sector. First, there is a movement to enact laws that substantially protect artists' creative freedom and rights, related to the issue of excluding artists from support based on political ideology. Second, in areas where art is becoming industrialized and market circulation is becoming more active,

Artists Welfare Act

1) Overview

2011 2012 2011
The "Artist Welfare Act" enacted in 2011 and promulgated in 2012 is a law aimed at legally protecting the professional status and rights of artists and promoting their welfare. Artists rarely establish typical employment contracts, making it difficult to receive protection under labor laws, and since many are young people with working abilities, it is also not easy to receive welfare benefits such as minimum living support. Due to incidents such as the case of the late screenplay writer-director Choi Go-eun, who tragically passed away in 2011, the awareness of the need to guarantee the rights of artists who are in welfare blind spots increased, ultimately leading to the enactment of this law

UNESCO (United Nations Educational, Scientific and Cultural Organization) established and disseminated the "Recommendation concerning the Status of the Artists" in 1980. This recommendation was based on the recognition that art enriches a society's cultural identity and spiritual heritage, and contributes to social integration, emphasizing that the state should

It should establish infrastructure such as museums and art galleries, support job creation for artists in public and private sectors, and establish legal protective measures to realize the International Labor Organization (ILO) labor standards (including working hours, paid leave, weekly rest, etc.). 4 ) 4 ) ^(4)){ }^{4)}

Artists' welfare receives broad ideological support, but is a very challenging area to implement in policy. As UNESCO itself points out, there is no agreed-upon standard for who professional artists are, what employment forms are typical in the arts sector, or where the boundaries of freelance and self-employed work lie. This is because working environments differ dramatically across sectorssuch as the fine arts with many individual creations, popular arts with more group activities, or fields with extensive outsourcing work. Additionally, the forms of cultural administrative departments and the distribution of authority between central and local governments vary by country, making comparisons difficult

Industrial accident insurance is a social insurance managed by the state to prepare for industrial accidents such as workers' injuries, illnesses, disabilities, or deaths due to work-related reasons. The original industrial accident insurance was designed for general workers with guaranteed working hours and workplace based on employment contracts, and the state collects insurance premiums from employers through the Workers' Compensation Service. In the case of artists, they rarely have stable employment contracts and have characteristics of intermittent employment relationships based on project-specific contracts, placing them in a blind spot of the industrial accident insurance system. If an actor is injured during performance rehearsal, a staff member is injured while making stage sets, or a writer is injured during reporting activities, it becomes entirely the individual's responsibility. 10 ) 10 ) ^(10)){ }^{10)}

III.
III. Conclusion

. , , , .
Over the past time, we have briefly examined the recent legislative trends in the cultural arts sector. On one side, welfare systems and support measures are being designed from a perspective that views cultural artists as somewhat marginalized groups, while on the other side, discussions are taking place on typical economic regulations such as consumer protection, fair trade, and market transparency, based on the premise that the industry has grown to a certain scale.
, . . . , .
Due to the unique characteristics of the cultural arts sector, it is challenging to directly apply existing welfare and economic laws. Artists should not be protected simply because they are socially vulnerable. Rather, they are guaranteed a certain level of status due to their public contribution to enhancing community welfare through cultural advancement. Moreover, while regulations for establishing fair trading order are necessary, they should not suppress artistic and creative freedom, and it must also be considered that most transactions occur under severe imbalances of capital and information.
- - , . . , .
Previously, cultural arts administration often occurred in the form of budget allocation and management-supervision of major associations and organizations. However, as the market's self-reliance foundation strengthens and demands for institutional correction of unfair practices increase, the administrative weight is likely to tilt towards regulatory legislation. The law on art distribution discussed briefly in this text is a good example. While there was previously a strong perception that art was not a regulated industry, as the importance of art exhibition, appreciation, and collection in daily life has grown, a consensus has been reached that creative and distribution processes should be separated, and at least distribution should be subject to some level of industrial regulation.
, . , , , , , .
The true meaning of industrial promotion that the government can undertake is to establish rational standards, guarantee predictability, and encourage a virtuous cycle of market actors' autonomy and responsibility. Although currently only various possibilities are being discussed, it is anticipated that in the future, the necessity of applying administrative legal methodologies such as licensing, certification, administrative sanctions, administrative investigations, and dispute mediation in the cultural arts sector will increase.

    Literature can be considered the starting point of all art and the cornerstone of culture as a whole, which provides "didactic functions" that convey life lessons and truths to readers, as well as "pleasurable functions" that offer high-quality spiritual enjoyment. Literature deals with individuals not only in their personal lives but also as members of a community, thereby enabling individuals to grow as social beings who live within a community. In this sense, literature plays a critical role in healing the recent prevalent trend of disregarding humanity and materialistic values, and in reviving the humanistic spirit within the community

    However, due to the preference for entertainment with the development of the internet and smartphones, the decline of reading culture with the development of media and video media, the sluggish sales of literary books due to a social atmosphere that prioritizes material things, and the fact that literature-related departments, including creative writing departments, are being prioritized for closure during university restructuring, the conditions for promoting literature in our society are increasingly deteriorating, and there are no legal grounds or integrated national institutions related to literature.

    Therefore, this proposed legislation aims to systematically support the literary field and strengthen the foundation of Korean culture by establishing legal grounds for promoting literature as an independent genre.
