We thank all three reviewers for their engagement and recognition of our task-level mapping, analysis of interviewee perspectives, and socio-technical takeaways for future work. Below, we first address the shared concern about sample size and composition, then respond to individual reviewer suggestions on contribution positioning, future research directions, and locally deployable models.

We appreciate that all reviewers acknowledged our transparent approach to discussing sample limitations. Although our sample of fourteen aligns with Caine (2016) and Hennink and Kaiser (2022) (n=9–17), we recognize that the variation across jurisdictions would benefit from a larger sample. We thank Reviewer pv3S for locating Baćak et al. (2024) and Webster et al. (2023). Baćak et al. recruited 78 indigent defense counsels, compensated participants $50 [3]; Webster et al. recruited 22 prosecutors, 17 defense attorneys, and 10 judges without mentioning compensation [4]. Our topic posed distinct recruitment challenges compared to both: Baćak et al. studied occupational stress and Webster et al. examined COVID-19 adaptation, which are broadly relatable across offices. However, AI adoption remains controversial. Office leadership was not always supportive of participation in AI-related research, even for a descriptive study, likely due to ongoing public discourse about AI risks in the legal profession. Our institutional restrictions on compensating government employees further constrained outreach. Taken together, these barriers made recruitment exceptionally difficult: securing our fourteen participants required six months of sustained effort.

We appreciate Reviewer 2AdY's suggestions and will continue recruiting through the revision period. We are candid, however, that reaching N=25–30 by the deadline is unlikely. That said, our current study already offers substantial insight. As a qualitative study, convergence of themes across participants is the appropriate gauge of sufficiency [5,6] and we observed consistent convergence: participants across career stages and jurisdictions raised the same core concerns and opportunities. We will also incorporate public statements from practicing defenders, such as NJ Public Defender Jennifer Sellitti's remark at end of 2025, "technology will not define the future of public defense. Our lawyers will" to further contextualize our findings.

We also note that response rates are a criterion for probability-based survey sampling, not purposive qualitative research, where sample adequacy is assessed differently [5,6]. Our sample covers federal, state, and county jurisdictions, office-employed and contract-based attorneys, and varied career stages. Five of the six law professors actively participate in public defense through clinical programs; the sixth has over 20 years of public defense experience and retired only months before our interview.

Reviewer pv3S noted that evidence investigation being the most benefited area is rather straightforward: we think this depends on the audience. When we asked an NLP researcher why evaluation benchmarks for multimodal evidence investigation barely exist while suppressing hallucinations in legal research attracts considerable attention [7,8], they responded, "That is because hallucinations are a collective problem nobody knows the answer to yet, while long-form video model evaluation is such a niche area. It's laborious, and it isn't clear if you can get a job out of it." We hope that our discussion provides an anchor for AI researchers who care about access to justice, presents research opportunities to strengthen public defense, and helps justify access to justice work as public interest [9,10].

Reviewer pv3S also raises an important point about prosecutor-defender resource disparities in AI access. This theme emerged in our interviews and is briefly discussed in Section 5.3, but our data are not comprehensive enough to support strong claims. We agree this warrants dedicated future work—ideally following Webster et al.'s model of including prosecutors' and judges' perspectives [4], or through a quantitative study examining whether justice gaps in AI access persist across jurisdictions. We will frame this explicitly as a key direction for future work.

Lastly, we are grateful that Reviewer 2AdY found our discussion of locally deployable models compelling. When we drafted the paper, local models were widely considered to have significantly lower performance than commercial foundation models and were difficult to deploy. Recent developments in open-weight models and on-device inference reinforce the practical viability of this pathway, and we look forward to enriching this discussion in Section 5.4.
